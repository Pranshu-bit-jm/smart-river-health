What the pH scale means

pH measures how acidic or basic water is. The neutral point is 7.0. For most rivers and lakes we use a practical healthy range: 6.5 – 8.5. Values far outside this range can harm aquatic life or indicate pollution.

pH Ranges & Practical Impact

RangeLabelPractical impact
0 – 3Very acidicHighly corrosive; toxic to nearly all aquatic life. Immediate attention required.
4 – 5AcidicHarmful to many freshwater species; increases metal solubility (lead, copper).
5.5 – 6.4Slightly acidicMay stress sensitive species; consider monitoring and buffers.
6.5 – 8.5Neutral / HealthySuitable for most aquatic life and safe for recreational contact with normal precautions.
8.6 – 9.5Slightly alkalineMinor effects; watch for ammonia toxicity in warm water.
9.6 – 11AlkalineCan harm fish and plants; consider filtration or dilution methods.
11 – 14Very alkalineCorrosive and hazardous; avoid contact and notify authorities if found in the field.

Quick tips

  • Always use a calibrated meter or fresh test strips for accuracy.
  • Record temperature with your pH reading — pH is temperature-sensitive.
  • If pH is outside safe bounds (<6 or >9), avoid using the water and retest or send sample to a lab.
  • Small sample neutralization can be attempted (see pH Care page) — never treat large rivers without authority approval.
Sources: WHO drinking water guidelines; US EPA & USGS educational pages on pH and aquatic ecosystems.
